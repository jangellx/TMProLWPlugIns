PowerSensor Information

The Spaceball device is a true 3D input device that detects the slightest fingertip pressure applied to it and resolves the pressure into X, Y, and Z translations and rotations, moving your 3D images instantaneously and simultaneously. This provides intuitive, interactive six degrees-of-freedom control of 3D graphical images and objects.

Operating Modes

Using the Spaceball device is natural and intuitive. There are two basic ways to move objects with the Spaceball device:

  • Manipulate the Spaceball® PowerSensor® ball as if you were holding the 3D model in your hand - this is Object Mode. Push left and the image moves left. Push right and it moves right. Lift up or push down and the image moves accordingly. Push away and or pull forward on the PowerSensor (the ball shaped portion of the controller) and the image responds. Twist in any direction and the image rotates in that direction.
  • Manipulate the Spaceball PowerSensor ball as if it were your head or a camera - this is Eyepoint Mode or Camera Mode. Push left and the scene moves to the right. Push right and the scene moves to the left. Lift up and the scene moves down. Push down and the scene moves up.
You can apply any of these actions at the same time, causing the image to move as you move.

The patented Spaceball PowerSensor technology is based on a mechanism which separates a combined force and torque into six forces and then uses optical mask sensing technology to detect the resulting displacements. A hollow outer ball ‘floats’ in place on six leaf springs, each with a ball tip that fits into a corresponding slot in the outer ball. As the outer ball is pushed and/or twisted the leaf springs are deflected so as to resist the movement. The LED/detector pairs measure the deflection of each leaf spring.

Optical Sensor SpacerspacerspacerspPowerSensor Axes

In the Optical Sensor graphic, the purple object is the LED. The green object to the right is the photo sensor. The red square on the photo sensor blocks the light from the LED. An optical sensor is located at the end of each leaf spring.

Each leaf spring has a molded on optical mask that blocks the amount of light reaching the detector. At rest half the light is blocked. Spring deflection in one direction lets more light through and deflection in the other direction blocks more light. The amount of light is measured to determine the spring deflection.

Adding a pair of readings (from both ends of an axis) yields the force vector. Subtracting one of the pair from the other yields the torque vector. For example, in the diagram above F2+F5 yields the force along the red axis. F2-F5 yields the torque about the blue axis.

Data Supplied

The Spaceball provides full, simultaneous six-axis movement in any and all directions. The Spaceball PowerSensor delivers 10 bits of digital precision. The diagrams below show the orientation of Translation and Rotation axes on the Spaceball PowerSensor. Following the diagrams, there are two charts explaining the data range of each axis, and the axis ‘meaning’ in both 6D Navigation (Eyepoint Control) and 6D Object Control (Object Movement).

It is crucial to a good implementation of the Spaceball and your application that the movement resulting from pressures on the PowerSensor be as smooth and instantaneous as possible. Please use the diagrams and charts below when adding support for the Spaceball to your application.

The Spaceball can return data in the 32767 to –32768 range on each axis. The usable range tends to be in the 10000 to –10000 range. In addition to the axis data, the Spaceball also returns the elapsed time since the last event. This is called the 'period' and is measured in 1/16ths of a millisecond.

Why ArbitraryAxisToMatrix

The Spaceball produces both a translation and a rotation vector simultaneously as a user pushes, pulls, or torques the PowerSensor (as is shown in the diagram below). The translation vector represents the force the user applies to move the Spaceball. The rotations returned from the Spaceball represent the vector about which the user is applying a torque.

Applying a Rotation to the PowerSensor

The translation vector is fairly easy to interpret. The three components (X, Y, and Z) of the translation vector can be applied in the same manner as similar data from the keyboard or mouse is applied to the viewing transform. The rotation vector is a different situation. Applying the rotation vector as individual parts will not give the same result as rotation about the vector (see below). If the data is applied individually the user will notice a “wobble” when performing a rotation.
